# Transmission Media

Transmission media refers to the physical or wireless path that data travels through between devices.

There are two main categories:
- Wireless
- Wired

---

## 1. Wireless Transmission Media

Wireless uses electromagnetic waves to transmit data through the air (no physical cable).

### 802.11 (Wi-Fi)

- Standard for wireless local area networks (WLANs)
- Operates mainly on 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, and 6 GHz bands
- Used in homes, offices, coffee shops
- Limited range compared to wired
- Susceptible to interference (walls, microwaves, other Wi-Fi networks)

Pros:
- Mobility
- Easy deployment

Cons:
- Lower security if misconfigured
- Interference and signal degradation

---

### Cellular (4G / 5G)

- Uses radio towers for wide-area communication
- Used by mobile devices
- Covers large geographic areas
- Managed by telecom providers

Pros:
- Wide coverage
- Good for remote access and backup connectivity

Cons:
- Latency may be higher than wired
- Dependent on signal strength

---

### Satellite

- Data transmitted to satellites orbiting Earth
- Used in rural or remote areas
- High latency due to long travel distance (especially geostationary satellites)

Pros:
- Global coverage
- Useful where no other infrastructure exists

Cons:
- High latency
- Weather interference

---

## 2. Wired Transmission Media

Wired media uses physical cables to transmit data.

---

### Fiber Optic Cable

- Uses light pulses to transmit data
- Extremely high speed and bandwidth
- Very long distance capability
- Immune to electromagnetic interference (EMI)

Types:
- Single-mode fiber (long distance, core networks)
- Multi-mode fiber (shorter distance, buildings)

Pros:
- Fastest speeds
- Secure and reliable

Cons:
- More expensive
- Fragile compared to copper

---

### Coaxial Cable

- Copper cable with shielding
- Used in cable internet and older networks
- Resistant to signal interference

Pros:
- Durable
- Good for broadband delivery

Cons:
- Less common in modern LAN setups
- Slower than fiber

---

### DAC (Direct Attach Copper)

- Short copper cable with connectors on both ends
- Common in data centers
- Used to connect switches to servers

Pros:
- Low latency
- Cost-effective for short distances

Cons:
- Very short range (typically under 7 meters)

---

## Key Differences

Wireless:
- Mobility
- Subject to interference
- Easier installation

Wired:
- More stable
- Higher speed (especially fiber)
- More secure and reliable

---

## Tip

Understand:
- Which medium is best for long distance (Fiber)
- Which medium has highest latency (Satellite)
- Which is used for short, high-speed data center connections (DAC)
- Which supports mobility (Wi-Fi / Cellular)

Always think: speed, distance, interference, and use case.
